# 1 · CUSTOMER OPERATING MODELS

A semiconductor purchase is an underwriting decision. The component's price is trivial against the cost of the component failing: a three-dollar part in a sixty-thousand-dollar vehicle, a tool that gates a twenty-billion-dollar fab, a license seat standing between a design team and a multi-million-dollar respin [F: recall charges, fab capex, and advanced-node mask costs are all on the public record]. Every buying behavior that matters follows from this asymmetry. Customers are not shopping for components; they are deciding whose risk to carry. Sellers who lead with performance are answering a question the buyer asked fifth.

The buyer is not a company. It is a set of people with careers, and the purchase must survive all of them:

- The **design engineer** proposes. Their incentive is that their block works and their schedule holds. Championing a new supplier spends personal credibility; what they need from a seller is not enthusiasm but ammunition to defend the choice upward. They almost never decide alone [O].
- The **system architect** partitions. Whole component categories are designed in or out at partition time, months before any evaluation. A seller absent from the architecture conversation has already lost sockets no field effort will recover [O].
- The **program manager** de-risks. Graded on schedule, not on BOM elegance, they will reinstate the incumbent at design freeze unless the swap has a cost — an executive who would have to be told, a quality team that already signed, a commercial term tied to the socket. Most losses recorded as surprises were decided here [O].
- The **quality engineer** vetoes. Audits, qualification data, and change-control discipline pass through this desk, and its memory is the relationship's balance sheet. A quality organization audits only what it might buy; an audit request is the truest buying signal in the funnel [O].
- The **sourcing organization** admits and prices. It owns the approved-vendor list, runs the negotiation calendar, and sponsors second sources as policy, not betrayal. It is graded on documented savings and on the absence of surprises — which means certainty is a product it will pay for [O].

Engineering proposes, quality qualifies, sourcing admits, program management de-risks, executives approve structure. A seller can lose at any of the five, and each "no" requires a different correction. A loss review that has not identified which "no" occurred has not finished [O].

What follows reconstructs the operating models a commercial organization actually meets. Two variables generate most of the differences: the failure-cost ratio and the time horizon. Everything else is detail.

**Automotive OEM.** Decision process: platform programs on three-to-five-year cycles; the OEM owns the specification and the approved-vendor list even where a Tier-1 executes the purchase. Buying center: architecture and platform engineering propose; central quality and purchasing govern; program boards approve. Incentives and KPIs: launch on date, zero field failures, scheduled cost reduction. Qualification logic: the industry's heaviest — component standards, production-process approval, functional-safety work products, site audits; qualification time is set by procedure and physics and cannot be sold around [F: standard test durations are published]. Career risk: recall-shaped; conservatism is rational, not cultural. Commercial constraints: annual price-down rituals, dual-source policies, ten-to-fifteen-year supply expectations. Selection logic: quality and longevity above supply security, above roadmap fit, above performance, above price. Switching cost: extreme once qualified — requalification plus safety-case rework. Horizon: the platform's life and its successor's.

**Automotive Tier-1.** The same regime one level down, with a defining twist: the Tier-1 sits between the OEM's directives and its own margin. Where the OEM has directed a component, the Tier-1 executes; where it has discretion, it optimizes cost and reuse across multiple OEM programs. Sellers must read which regime governs each socket — a directed socket is won at the OEM, a discretionary one at the Tier-1, and effort aimed at the wrong altitude is wasted [O]. The Tier-1's own KPI pressure (program margin, warranty exposure) makes it simultaneously more price-sensitive and more risk-averse than its OEM.

**Industrial automation.** Fragmented demand, thousands of build-to-last products, and an engineer who frequently is the buying center. Incentives: robustness, longevity, and not having to think about the component again for fifteen years. Qualification: segment certifications plus the buyer's own conservatism; less formal than automotive, no less real. Career risk: the product in the field for two decades with their name on the design. Constraints: small volumes per socket, distribution-mediated purchasing, acute sensitivity to end-of-life behavior — an obsolescence surprise is the design-out generator of the segment [O]. Selection logic: longevity, then robustness, then support, then price. Switching cost: high in practice — firmware, characterization, and the engineer's scarce attention. Horizon: longest in the industry outside aerospace.

**Medical.** The regulatory shadow governs: a supplier's process change can trigger the customer's re-filing, so change control is not a quality detail but the substance of the relationship. Buying center: engineering proposes, quality and regulatory hold effective veto. Selection logic: continuity above everything; a datasheet advantage cannot compensate for change-control doubt. Switching cost: extreme — requalification plus regulatory risk. Horizon: product generations measured in decades. The commercial consequence: notification discipline and documentation depth are the primary sales instruments, and a mishandled change notice at one medical account taxes every other [O].

**Aerospace and defense.** Program-office governance, provenance and traceability requirements, qualification to military and space standards, and contract structures administered rather than negotiated. Career risk is mission-shaped; supply expectations run to decades, with obsolescence management as a standing discipline. Selection logic: assurance, traceability, longevity; price is a compliance item. Access is gated by clearances and eligibility before any commercial merit applies — a parallel commercial universe with its own channel and calendar [F: the regime; O: the practice].

**Consumer electronics.** The inverted regime: schedule is everything, cost is the product, and the buying center is split between an OEM that specifies and an ODM or EMS that purchases. Incentives: hitting the build date at target cost. Qualification: light and fast. Career risk: a slipped launch, a line-down event. Constraints: quarterly cost-downs, contractual step-downs, substitution rights — "or equivalent" in the bill of materials is a standing threat that converts a specification win into nothing if the purchasing leg is not separately won [O]. Selection logic: cost, availability, time-to-market, then performance. Switching cost: low to moderate, by design. Horizon: a product generation of six to eighteen months.

**Enterprise OEM.** Server, networking, storage, and instrument makers: professional procurement, moderate qualification, multi-year platforms, and a buying center where engineering influence remains strong but sourcing runs structured negotiations. Selection logic balances roadmap alignment and supply assurance against cost; dual-sourcing is standard where the architecture permits. The segment rewards roadmap credibility — these customers plan products around suppliers' futures and punish missed generations by designing flexibility against the offender [O].

**Cloud and hyperscale.** Engineering selects bottom-up at fleet scale; central sourcing wields dual-source policy and the standing option of internal silicon as pricing instruments [F: internal-silicon programs are public]. Incentives: fleet economics — performance per dollar per watt — and availability; career risk is fleet downtime and roadmap dependence on a supplier who stumbles. Qualification is informal in name and brutal in substance: software maturity and validation at fleet scale are the real regime, and they cannot be shortcut [O]. Selection logic: total cost of ownership, then roadmap and supply certainty, then price as structure (multi-year commitments rather than unit discounts). Switching cost: concentrated in software and operations; high where stacks are deep, deliberately reduced by the customer's own abstraction investments. Horizon: two to four years per platform, with the customer's internal alternative always priced in. This customer is simultaneously buyer, potential competitor, and sponsor of the seller's rivals; an account plan that models only the first role misreads the account [O].

**ODM and EMS.** Procurement-dominant, margin-thin, schedule-absolute. They execute another company's approved-vendor list where it is specific and substitute where it says "or equivalent." Incentives: margin on the build and the absence of line-down events. Selection logic: cost, availability, terms. The commercial consequence for sellers: the ODM/EMS is a second funnel, not a formality — spec wins die here quietly, and availability failures are punished with substitution faster than any other customer type acts on anything [O].

**Distribution.** A customer and a channel at once. The distributor buys inventory risk and sells two different services: fulfillment, at thin margin, and demand creation, at protected margin where registration systems reward the branch engineer who created the design-in. Incentives: inventory turns and protected economics. Selection logic for line cards: breadth, support quality, protection credibility. The distributor's field engineers are rentable campaign capacity; whether they spend their hours creating demand for a supplier is decided by that supplier's registration discipline, and a squeezed registration reallocates the bench to competitors with a lag of about two years and no announcement [O].

Two closing disciplines. First, the buyer's scorecard usually exists in writing and is usually obtainable — most large customers share supplier-evaluation criteria with suppliers who ask [O]. Selling without it is answering an exam without reading the questions. Second, what cannot be known: the buyer's internal politics at any moment. What can be known is the structure those politics run inside — the five roles, their incentives, their calendar — and structure is enough to act on. The seller's task is not to predict the buyer's mind but to remove, role by role, every reason any of the five would have to say no.
