# 3 · COMMERCIAL ARCHETYPES

The same commercial machines recur across the industry's history under different logos. Each archetype below is a stable configuration of the mechanisms in Module 2 — a particular answer to the question of which customers to serve, with which moat, funded how. Companies are cited as evidence of the configuration, not as its definition; the archetypes predate today's examples and will outlive them. For each: why it exists, its economics, where it wins, where it fails, and what eventually breaks it.

**The Catalog Machine.** Exists because a large share of semiconductor demand is thousands of shallow sockets whose buyers value availability, breadth, and price over co-design. Economics: collateral, boards, models, and stock amortized across tens of thousands of parts and customers make the marginal design-in nearly free; a direct digital channel converts the long tail without human touch and yields unmediated demand data that feeds capacity planning [F: the visible infrastructure and stated channel rationale of the largest analog catalogs]. Manufacturing cost position is the engine — breadth pricing is only safe from above a structural cost advantage [I]. Wins: numerous shallow sockets, availability-decided purchases, the industrial tail. Fails: performance-gated sockets expecting co-design; the machine's portal is read as neglect there [O]. Breaks when: clones reach pin-compatible parity at the tail faster than the catalog refreshes, or the cost moat erodes — the machine's defense is perpetual manufacturing reinvestment and tiered concession of indefensible parts [O]. A longevity variant exists: a catalog whose promise is never obsoleting anything, aimed at buyers whose enemy is change [F: the stated practice]; it trades growth ceiling for permanent loyalty.

**The Application Machine.** Exists because some sockets are hard enough that the customer buys a co-designer, not a component. Economics: heavy application-engineering investment recovered through value pricing on decade-life franchise sockets; the margin funds the depth that defends the margin [I from the visible margin-and-field-intensity pairing at precision franchises]. Its capital doctrine is usually curation — buying adjacent franchises whole, because qualified positions and engineer mindshare cannot be organically compressed [F: the serial franchise-acquisition record]. Wins: performance-gated, qualification-heavy applications with rising content. Fails: without worthy targets — the machine idles on easy sockets; and it cannot cover breadth. Breaks when: the application transitions (a topology or architecture shift resets the accumulated knowledge) or when integration dissolves the socket class entirely; the defense is owning the transition rather than defending the configuration [O].

**The Platform Machine.** Exists where a coordination point can be held — a layer every complement must meet. Economics: mindshare and complement subsidy carried for years before revenue; recovered through category pricing once the network closes; roadmap cadence as pricing collateral; concentrated accounts negotiated on futures and supply [F: the compute-platform record — ecosystem seeded half a decade pre-revenue, later priced through generations]. Wins: any category where selection is bottom-up and the workload recurs across markets. Fails: where buyers are operations organizations qualifying top-down — the gravity has nothing to act on [O]. Breaks when: a coalition standardizes the held layer, an abstraction above commoditizes it, or a discontinuity resets the complements; the machine's paradox is that its own success recruits the coalition [O].

**The Ecosystem Machine.** The platform machine's open sibling: it standardizes the interface *above* the layer it owns, giving enablement away so that building on it becomes the default. Economics: the free layer (design kits, reference flows, developer boards, communities) removes friction for everyone and commoditizes nothing the owner sells; demand consolidates on the enabled layer beneath [F: the foundry enablement model; the microcontroller community model]. Wins: where the owner genuinely controls the layer below — then neutrality is strategy. Fails: attempted without that ownership — then the generosity enables competitors [O]. Breaks when: the owner is tempted to compete with those it enables (the neutrality asset does not survive the first conflict) or when the enabled layer itself is leapfrogged [O].

**The Equipment Machine.** Exists because process capability is sold as capital goods whose failure cost is a fab's schedule. Economics: long qualification campaigns ending in process-of-record positions that annuitize for a decade; an installed base that converts into service, spares, upgrades, and telemetry — a second business growing inside the first [F: disclosed service-segment structures across the equipment majors]; resident engineering as the sales force. Variants ascend with irreplaceability: the depth specialist annuitizes the highest-intensity steps; the breadth integrator sells co-optimized sequences; the information position prices inspection against yield economics; and at the summit, the shared-fate form — customers co-funding the supplier's next generation because their alternative is no roadmap [F: the customer co-investment record in lithography]. Wins: wherever process risk dominates price. Fails: between transitions against an entrenched process-of-record — patience is the only strategy there. Breaks when: an architecture transition is misread — full conviction at the wrong window, or hedging at the right one; the machine's history turns on a handful of such calls [F: the immersion-era outcome].

**The Foundry Machine.** Exists because design and manufacturing separate cleanly when trust makes the interface safe. Economics: capacity as a service, priced on node leadership and underwritten by prepayments; a yield-learning engine whose speed is the real product; and a founding constraint that is also the moat — never compete with customers, because neutrality is what permits unlimited customer depth [F: the doctrine and the concentration it enabled]. Wins: as the default home of everyone else's innovation. Fails: where the operator holds product ambitions — the conflict taxes every relationship [O]. Breaks when: node economics stop clearing for enough customers, or geography reprices the whole position — risks priced by customers, governments, and the operator alike [F: the disclosed diversification programs].

**The Distribution Machine.** Exists because suppliers cannot economically touch the long tail and buyers in it need engineering help anyway. Economics: thin fulfillment margin at scale plus protected demand-creation margin where registration systems reward the branch engineer who created a design-in; inventory risk carried in exchange for both [O]. Wins: fragmented mid-tail markets, regional density, launch amplification. Fails: at the design-heavy top of the market, and wherever suppliers treat it as logistics. Breaks when: suppliers disintermediate (the direct-channel conversion) or squeeze registration economics — the bench quietly reallocates to competitors with a two-year lag; and when catalog e-commerce absorbs the fulfillment layer, leaving only demand creation to defend the margin [O].

**The Software Lock-in Machine.** Exists because design methodology, once learned by an engineering population, defends itself. Economics: ratable subscriptions on tools whose sign-off authority gates the customer's largest expenditures; renewal expansion; embedded application engineers amortized by lifetime value; the skill base — careers built on the flow — as the deepest switching cost in the industry [F: the recurring-revenue structure and its history]. Wins: continuously, quietly, for decades. Fails: nowhere ordinary — which is the warning. Breaks only at methodology shifts, the sole events that have ever displaced the archetype's leaders; the only proven defense is self-disruption at a scale that would frighten the incumbent if a rival announced it, and the recurring failure is cannibalization throttled to gesture to protect the old economics [O; F: the incumbents' visible investment against their own seat model].

**The National Champion Machine.** Exists wherever industrial policy converts sovereignty into demand. Economics: eligibility-backed revenue — localization scoring and substitution mandates create customers whose approved-vendor mathematics favor the domestic supplier — plus patient public capital that de-risks capacity no market signal would fund [F: the funding programs and procurement preferences on the record across several eras and regions]. The archetype is not new and not regional: earlier generations built national memory and equipment industries under the same configuration before graduating into ordinary competitors [F: the historical record in more than one country]. Wins: at home, at the qualification on-ramp — the buyer wants to qualify the champion and will co-develop it. Fails: where accumulated craft, ecosystems, and installed trust cannot be mandated — the gap closes on wafers and years, not decrees [O]. Breaks when: the policy support outlives the discipline it was meant to build — subsidy without graduation produces capacity without competitiveness; or succeeds so well that the champion graduates and the archetype dissolves into one of the others [I]. For incumbents facing the archetype: uniform defense is irrational; the rational posture is tiered — defend where mechanism gaps are real, concede in writing where they are not, and never mistake the champion's current product for its trajectory [O].

Three uses for the taxonomy. Diagnosis: identify a company's archetype and its position within the archetype's lifecycle, and most of its behavior becomes predictable. Self-knowledge: most failed strategies are one archetype's move executed by another's machine — a catalog running a co-design campaign, an application machine chasing breadth, a platform pricing like a component vendor. Foresight: archetypes break at knowable points; watching for the breaking conditions of one's own machine is the strategy function's least glamorous and highest-value work.
