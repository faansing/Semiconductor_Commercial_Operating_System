# 2 · COMMERCIAL MECHANISMS

Commercial advantage in semiconductors is built from a small number of recurring mechanisms. They appear in every segment, every geography, and every era under different names. A reader who can recognize them in an unfamiliar company understands that company's commercial position better than its own marketing does. Each mechanism below is described the same way: what it is, how it is built, how it decays, and what evidence reveals its state.

**Trust systems.** Incumbency is compounded de-risking. Every passed audit, honest errata disclosure, fair allocation, and disciplined change notice becomes a line in the customer's risk file that discounts the next sale; the accumulated file is the incumbent's real product [O]. Trust is built through artifacts, not assurances — characterization data, drift studies, containment reports delivered within a day or two of an excursion — and it is asymmetric: accumulation takes years per account, and a single concealed problem reverses a decade in a quarter [O]. The post-shortage record made the mechanism visible at industry scale: suppliers who allocated by published rules hold scorecard positions that price into RFQs years later; suppliers who allocated capriciously collected dual-source mandates [I from disclosed sourcing-policy changes]. Decay signals: scorecard drift, invitation lag, forecast feeds closing. What cannot be bought: time. Trust systems are the mechanism most resistant to capital, which is why attackers rent them where possible — through distributors, design houses, and reference customers whose trust transfers partially [O].

**Qualification systems.** Qualification converts a customer's risk aversion into a wall around the incumbent. Every standard passed, every production-process approval signed, every process-of-record granted is capital: it was expensive to build, it depreciates only at platform transitions, and it must be climbed by every subsequent attacker [O]. Two commercial consequences follow. Qualification spend is investment, not cost of sales — subsidizing a customer's qualification effort (engineering support, test lots) buys a barrier amortized over the socket's life and every later defense of it [O]. And qualification timestamps are the most reliable dates in any forecast: audits and approvals are contractual events in a funnel otherwise made of intentions [O]. Decay: walls reset at transitions — architecture changes, node changes, regulatory changes — which is why incumbents fear discontinuities more than competitors, and why attackers should budget patience between transitions and violence at them [O].

**Switching-cost systems.** Switching costs are engineered, not incidental, and they stack: hardware requalification; firmware and software embedding; formal certification; workflow and skill lock-in, where an engineering population's accumulated fluency defends a tool for decades; ecosystem network effects, where third parties' investments become the incumbent's moat; and co-specialization, where joint roadmaps and shared assets make separation irrational for both sides [O]. Each level is roughly an order harder to displace than the one below [O]. The design consequence: every product decision should be examined for the level it adds — pin and firmware compatibility with one's own prior generation is retention; compatibility with a competitor's part is attack, because it lets the customer hold an option open at low cost until the incumbent stumbles [O]. The upper levels cannot be purchased late at any price; they are accumulated across product generations or not at all. Decay signal, reliable across decades: an incumbent that begins charging for what was free — tools, support, compatibility — is harvesting the mechanism rather than feeding it, and share loss follows with a lag of two to four years [O].

**Ecosystem systems.** An ecosystem is a subsidy flow directed at complement makers — tool vendors, software authors, module builders, educators — sustained until network effects make it self-funding [O]. Its economics are the hardest in commerce to govern because the spend is indefensible on any quarterly logic for years; that indefensibility is precisely the barrier to imitation, and strategically impatient organizations lose the mechanism to patient ones as a rule [I from the visible history: compute platforms seeded for half a decade before their markets existed [F: developer-program timelines]]. The operating discipline is free-layer integrity: give away the layer that forces demand for the paid layer, and never monetize it later — the tax on the free layer is self-harvesting [O]. The single best health metric is the time from a newcomer's curiosity to a working prototype, because engineers standardize on whatever worked first and carry the preference for a career [O]. Decay: rung inflation from inside, standardization coalitions from outside, and discontinuities that reset complements to zero — the only events that reopen an ecosystem-held category [O].

**Information systems.** Demand visibility is a commercial asset with owners, rights, and returns. Whoever sees true consumption earliest allocates capital and field resources best: a direct channel yields unmediated tail demand no distributor-mediated rival can see [F: the stated rationale of the industry's largest direct-channel conversion]; an installed base yields usage telemetry; a subscription model yields consumption itself. Position can also be informational: the inspection-and-metrology position sells information about everyone else's process and prices it against yield economics — evidence that measuring a system can out-earn operating it [F: the margin structure of the process-control segment]. Two disciplines govern the mechanism: data rights are negotiated assets (forecast feeds traded for allocation priority; telemetry clauses written into service contracts), and leading indicators must be back-tested against realized outcomes annually, because an instrument that does not learn is furniture [O]. Decay: customers ring-fencing procurement data, regional data walls, and — internally — instruments that no decision has cited in quarters.

**Capital-allocation systems.** How a company deploys capital is its commercial strategy stated honestly. Four coherent doctrines recur. Frontier-utility: capacity built ahead of demand, underwritten by customer prepayments and co-investment — rational where the asset is the moat and the customer's alternative to co-funding is no roadmap at all [F: customer co-investment programs and prepayment structures are on the record]. Compounder: own the cost curve or the information rent, convert to free cash flow, return it, and let a trained investor base become part of the operating model — the published capital-management framework reviewed identically for two decades is the visible form [F]. Curator: buy franchises and their qualified positions whole, keep manufacturing light, defend mix — rational where integration is a practiced craft and targets price below organic replacement [F: the serial franchise-acquisition record]. Orchestrator: deploy capital into other people's layers — supply reservations, complement subsidies, ecosystem equity — because the moat lives in the network, not the plant [F: disclosed purchase obligations and ecosystem investments at platform companies]. Each doctrine fails outside its conditions; imitating the posture without the preconditions — counter-cyclical conviction without the balance sheet and investor training, curation without integration craft — is the recurring fatal error [O].

**Learning systems.** Organizations that convert experience into calibration outperform organizations that convert it into folklore. The working parts: a loss corpus, coded within weeks while facts outrank politics, read quarterly at the top; base rates computed from closed history rather than accepted from optimism; back-tests that grade every forward instrument against what actually happened; and post-mortems that grade decisions on the evidence available at decision time, separating execution quality from decision quality — a good decision can fail on chance, a bad one can succeed on it, and an organization that confuses the two learns superstition [O]. The single most diagnostic number in a commercial organization is the distribution of coded loss causes; where "price" exceeds roughly two-fifths of it, the usual disease is missing value evidence in the field, not wrong list prices [O].

**Platform systems.** A platform holds the coordination point — the layer where hardware meets every framework, where design meets manufacturing, where a specification meets its implementations — and refuses to let that layer standardize while subsidizing everything around it [O]. Value migrates to the proprietary layers adjacent to standardized ones; the strategic contest is therefore over where the standard's boundary lands, and it is fought with reference implementations, buyer coalitions, and complement momentum rather than with committee votes [I from the recurring pattern: compatible extensions defeating superior clean-sheet architectures; interface standardization dissolving integrated rivals' advantages [F: the historical record of both]]. Roadmap cadence is a platform's pricing collateral: customers pay today partly for a credible tomorrow, and a missed generation is a pricing event before it is an engineering one [I]. Decay: boundary coalitions from below, abstraction layers from above, and the platform's own allocation behavior converting customers into sponsors of alternatives [O].

**Commercial feedback loops.** None of the mechanisms above is linear; each runs as a loop that compounds in both directions. The trust loop: fair behavior → scorecard position → earlier invitations → cheaper wins → volume → credibility. The franchise loop: the hardest socket won → application knowledge → cheaper adjacent wins → reputation among the application's engineers → inbound demand → the next hardest socket. The cadence loop: an on-time generation → roadmap credibility → design-ins priced on the future → revenue funding the next generation. The learning loop: honest data → real base rates → better allocation → higher win rates → credibility that keeps the data honest. Every loop reverses through the same linkages, and reversal is faster than accumulation [O]. The practical test for whether a loop exists rather than a story: its unit cost must fall with repetition — if the tenth win in an application costs what the second did, there is no franchise, only a sequence of sales [O]. Operating a commercial organization is largely the work of naming its loops, protecting them through downturns — when their inputs are cheapest and competitors cut theirs — and watching for the weak signals of reverse spin.
